Abstract

Large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable success, but their full potential can only be unlocked through careful alignment with human values and preferences. As human preferences are multi-dimensional, and individuals have their own preferences, recent techniques for multi-dimensional personalization have enabled models to generate responses personalized to explicit multi-dimensional preferences. However, human preferences are often implicit and thus hard to articulate, limiting the direct application of these techniques. To address this challenge, we propose a comparison-based active preference learning framework to capture implicit user preferences. Building on Bayesian inference, our work introduces a modified posterior update procedure to handle potential convergence issues in previous works and account for the possibility of incorrect user feedback. Also, inspired by generalized binary search, we employ an active query selection strategy to minimize the number of required comparisons. We validate our approach through theoretical analysis and experiments on language generation tasks, demonstrating its feedback efficiency and effectiveness in personalizing model responses.
